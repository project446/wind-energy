The Legacy of the Wind Wheel

Before the modern turbine, there was the Wind Wheel. Originating over 1,000 years ago, early wind wheels were vertical-axis machines used in Persia to grind grain and pump water. By the 12th century, the iconic horizontal-axis "Dutch Windmill" appeared in Europe, using massive canvas sails to harness the breeze.

Today, the "wheel" has evolved. While ancient windmills relied on drag (the wind pushing the blade), modern turbines use aerodynamic lift, allowing the blades to spin much faster than the wind itself, generating significantly more power.

Harnessing Kinetic Energy

Wind energy is a form of solar energy. The sun unevenly heats the atmosphere, creating pressure differences that result in wind. When this wind hits a turbine's blades, it converts kinetic energy into mechanical energy.

  • The Rotor: Captures the wind and begins the rotation.
  • The Gearbox: Speeds up the slow rotation of the blades to over 1,500 RPM.
  • The Generator: Uses magnetic fields to turn that rotation into electricity.
